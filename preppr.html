The Problem

Time and money are two of the largest barriers to developing healthier eating habits for Canadians who live alone. Taking into account food prep, grocery shopping, time to cook, and food wastage can be especially overwhelming.

Preppr takes the stress out of meal planning on a budget by providing affordable meals that are nutritious and delicious.

my contributions

This project consistent of three iterations where I was the User Experience Researcher, Business Analyst, and Product Designer respectively. While working in these three roles were required for the project, I mainly worked as a product designer to navigate through the design thinking of Preppr.

iteration one

User Experience Researcher

HMW facilitate healthy diets for people living alone?

target users

Preppr's target users are people who live alone/cook for one. As we only knew a limited amount of people who fit this persona, we turned to Reddit to ask for their relationships with food. With over 100 responses on different threads, we established three user groups.

testing

Is this product solving an actual problem?

Our initial goal for usability testing was to figure out whether Preppr was actually solving a problem for people who cook for one. Taking an agile approach with our design, we created assumptions based on what we believed users might want. With those in mind, I worked with the design team in order to figure out what aspects they wanted to test in addition to our user questions. From there, we found our users and started working on the script to be used later on. Even thought the testing was during Covid times, I was lucky enough to test our product in person with people who I live with.

key findings

From our testing, we found that certain functions we assumed would streamline cooking for one actually actually made it more difficult. With this in mind, we changed our product requirements list for the next iteration. Visually, our biggest takeaway from from our A/B testing of what people wanted to see on their home screen. User wise, we were able to deduce that the product has potential as people agreed with the pain points.

Mostly my concerns is picking foods that are quick to make, tasty, and that is not going to send me into bankruptcy hunting down some rare super food that xyz blog says is essential to living a healthy life. There's a difference between getting your daily fruit and veggies and killing yourself at the checkout counter.

iteration two

Business Analyst

How profitable is this application?

financial feasibility

Being as saturated as the food and drink market is, we had to figure out how we could differenciate our app as well as where our revenue streams would come from. Using a business to business (B2B) approach seemed more profitable than a business to consumer (B2C) one so we focused on developing multiple revenue streams through this. As we did not have any concrete numbers, I had to make some assumptions based on research I found. To combat this problem, I made a key next to our costs where when one number was changed, all of the financial statments would reflect the change.

To ensure financial stability, we not only wanted to work with existing grocery stores but also include features such as our own Preppr points and scraps buddy. While we do not get any direct revenue from consumers, having these differenciation features and multiple revenue streams can ensure longevity of our product.

iteration three

Product Designer

I picked up this project again after two iterations of design and testing in order to develop the high-fidelity prototype (I worked on the design in iteration one). While the initial designs had completed aspects of our product requirement list, the key features, branding, and cohesiveness just weren't there.

information architecture

Even up until the third iteration, the flow of our screens were still confusing to our users. To start fresh, the design team created a new flow based on our usability testing and our key features.

feature breakdowns and decisions

Recipe Exploration

For many people, finding new recipes is the best part of cooking. For others, it's more trouble than it's worth. It was important for us to give users different outlets to find what they're in the mood for whether it's spontaneous, ingredient driven, or they're just craving a certain dish.

discover and search

A simple and straightforward interaction that directly addresses our users' goals of finding a recipe

These pages were designed with simplicity in mind. Being front and center, users can browse through our explore page and spark inspiration for their current or future meals. If they're looking for specific items, our search function easily finds any meals they're in the mood for.

the scraps buddy

An interactive and customizable way to create leftover meals

For many of our users, using their existing ingredients is how they meal prep. Instead of having to individually Google ingredients, we created a fun and interactive method to find recipes not only based on your ingredients but also on your meal and cuisine.

past iterations

Previously, our Scraps Buddy lacked personality and so the interaction did not seem fun to use. While having the same technical functionality, the differentiation was not there from existing applications and neither was the customization. By making the process more fun, users will feel more inclined to continue using it, if not only for the functionality.

design system and branding

To kickstart the ideation process, we used a mindmap to start linking related words and ideas together. Once the blood was flowing, we split off and used a technique called crazy 8s where within 8 minutes, each of us had to come up with as many ideas as we could. Some of the ones I thought of included a myer-briggs style quiz with resources, a reproductive health application, and some sort of social media campaign. However, we had to focus on the big question: is this feasible?

Next Steps

Inventory, ingredient lifeline, loyalty program, partnerships

While Medley's characters and diagrams give the website personality, the execution still isn't what we hoped it would be. An appropriate next step for this project would be to allow for more user iteraction with the activities. This would not only make the site more engaging but given the right content, more educational. After that, implementing our map and creating our care pack would increase our value proposition.

Takeaways

Holistic design, heuristics, KPIs, emotional branding

While I really loved this project and the real world social impact it provided, one of my biggest qualms was the lack of LGBTQ+ members within our group members. We did a lot of research in order to overcome this problem but I wish we interview people in the community on top of our survey. That being said, our survey and research allowed me to impactfully tell the stories of LGBTQ+ sex. Another element I learned from this project is the importance of logical thinking and adaptability, especially in group settings. Knowing when to look at the big picture over focusing on minor details can save a lot of time and energy better spent elsewhere.

I hope that Medley sparks change in the sex education curriculum or that companies will provide better services to the LGBTQ+ community.
